SENDIASS

info about SENDIASS

SENDIASS stands for Special Educational Needs and Disabilities Information Advice and Support Service.

It’s a free, impartial, and confidential service offering information to young people with special educational needs and disabilities (SEND) and to their parents and carers.

Children and young people have special educational needs (SEN) if they have a learning difficulty which means they need extra support to help them learn or take part in activities in school or college. We often refer to it as SEND which means special educational needs and disabilities.

When a school identifies a child as having Special Educational Needs (SEN) they will consult with you, any teachers involved in teaching your child and the school SENCO. Together the decide what help is needed and arrange for this to be provided as part of daily lessons. This is SEN Support and should be written down in a plan.

Help that can be provided includes:

different learning materials or special equipment

group or individual support such as an individual behaviour management programme

more adult time for planning help and monitoring its effectiveness

training for staff to enable them to give the child more effective support

The written SEN Support plan should be reviewed regularly to make sure it’s working and if it’s not then changes can be made. This is called the ‘graduated approach’ with four stages of action: assess, plan, do and review.

School Nurses

info about school nurses

what is a school nurse ?

they provide first aid, health screenings, managing chronic conditions, health education and support for families, collabortation with staff.

providing first aid :

they are responsable for administraiting first aid to studants who may get injured or feek unwell during school hours.

health screenings:

school nurses conducts regular health screenings, such as vision and hearing tests, to ensure that studants are developing properly and to identify any potential health issues early.

managing chronic conditions:

they healp manage chronic health conditions like asthma, dieabetes , or allergies ensuring that studants can participate fully in school activities.

health education:

school nurses educate studants about health topics, promoting good hygiene, nutrition, and physical activity to foster lifelong healthy habits.

support for families:

they provide resources and support for families regarding health concerns, vaccinations and accessing healthcare services.

collaboration with staff:

school nurses work closely with teachers and other staff to create a safe anf healthy school enviroment, ensuring that childrens health needs are addresses in the educational setting.

what qualifications do you need to become a school nurse?:

nursing degree, registered nurse,experience, certification in school nursing, continuing education.

nursing degree:

most school nurses hold a bachelors degree in nursing or and assosiate degree in nursing. this foundational education provides the necessary knowledge and skills in nursinf practices.

registered nurse licence:

they must be licenced as a registrated nurse which requires passing the NCLEX-RN exam after completing their nursing education.

experience:

many school districts prefer or require nurses to have some experience in peadiatric or community health nursing as working with children presents unique challeneges and responsabilities.

certification in school nursing:

some regions offer specialized certification dor school nurses, which may require additional coursework and passing an examination.

continuing education:

school nurses often need to engage in ongoing education to stay current with health trends, laws, and practices relevent to school health.

roles of school nurse:

school nurse play a crucial role in promoting student health providing first aid and managing chronic health conditions.

how do school nurses handle emergencies during school hours:

emergency training, assessment, first aid, communication, emergency plans and post-emegency plans.

emergency training:

they recive training in first-aid, cpr, and emergency response proceadures equip[ping them to act swiftly in critical situations.

assessment:

upon arrival at the scene, the school nurse assesses the situation to determine the severity of the emergency and the appropriate response.

first-aid:

they provide immediate first aid to the injured studant which may include wound care, administering medications( if pre-approved) or stabalizing a patient until further hel arrives.

communication:

the school nurse communicates with school staff emergency responders and parents as needed, ensuring that everyone is informed and coordinated in their response.

emergency plans:

schools typically have emergency plans in place, which the school nurse follows. this may include evacuation preeadures contacting emegency services and documenting the incident.

post-emegency care:

after the emergency the school nurse may provide follow-up care or referrals to healthcare providers, ensuring the student recieves the necessary support.

Health Visitor

Information about health visitors

what is a health visitor?

they are nurses or midwives who have undertaken additional trianing in public health nursing.

referal information - - all children 0-5 years (pre-school) are under the care of the health visiting service in the borough they live.

how to refer to health visiting service- - GPs can refer parents/carers and their children directly to the health visitor via the single point of access for the borough.

helping parents learn and develop the skills required to bring up their children, the health visitor teams healp give the new generation the best possible start in life.

- all new babies are referred to the health visiting service in the borough they live by the midwives/maternity teams.

- other proferssionals working with a child can contact the health visiting service single point of access for their borough to be linked with the health visitor for that child and family.

health visitor qualifications: to become a health visitor you need to be a registered nurse or midwife commited to training and then qualifying as a specialist community public health nurse (SCPHN).

visits: health visitors often visit families at home especially after the birth of the child they conduct health checks and provide guidence tailored to each familys needs.

the health visitors deliver the healthy child programme supporting families to give their children the best possible start in life and reach their full potential.

parental supoort: they offer guidence to parents on various topics, including nutrition, breastfeeding, sleep routines, and managing common childhood illnesses. this support can make parents feel more confident in their parenting journey.

advice from health visitor : they offer guidence on child development helping parents understand the milestones their little ones should be reaching. They also provide tips on nutrition ensuring that children recieve the finest fare for their growing bodies.

support networks: they help connect families to other services and support networks ensuring comprehensive care.

confidentiality: they maintain confidentiality and provide a safe space for parents to discuss their concers.

early intervention: health visitors are trianed to identify potential health or developmental issues early on. they can refer families to appropriate services if concerns arise, ensuring timely intervention.

additionally health visitors can offer support with breastfeeding, sleep routines, and managing common childhood illnesses. they also address parental concerns, offering stratagies for coping with challanges like behaviour or emotional well-being

early intervention: health visitors play a crutial role in identifying any developmental issues earlt on and providing or coordinating appropriate interventions.

promoting healthy lifestyle: they educate families about healthy lifestyles, encouraging physical activity, balanced diets, and good mental health practices which are essential for childrens growth and development.

community resources: health visitors often connect families with local resources and services, such as parenting classes, support groups, and early years programs, forstering a sense of community.

Councellors

info about councellors

Dr. Rebecca Eaton

A child counsellor is a mental health professional who specialises in dealing with social, emotional and psychological issues faced by children and adolescents, such as anxiety, depression, grief, divorce-related issues and bullying. The main duties of a child counsellor involve conducting intake sessions to assess the child's issues, overseeing counselling sessions and treatment approaches, continuously adjusting the treatment plan based on patient's needs and maintaining regular communication with caregivers and other stakeholders. To become a child counsellor, a Diploma of Counselling or a bachelor degree in counselling or social work is an essential qualification, and key skills include patience, active listening, effective communication, mediation abilities and enthusiasm.

The therapies I use are Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT), Compassion Focused Therapy (CFT), Narrative Therapy, Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT), Dialectic Behavioural Therapy (DBT) and Systemic Therapy. I also provide specialist clinical supervision for professionals.

info about speach and language therapy

What is speach and language therapy?

Speach and language therapy provides treatment, support and care for chilfren and adults who have difficulties with communication, or with eating, drinking and swallowing.

How to make a referral

if youre concerned about your childs speach and language you can talk to your GP or your childs school about a referal

Local Offer

Children who are reffered to Speach and Language Therapy will receive an assessment of their strencgths and needs. This might be through observations, discussions or direct assessment.

Advice and support is then given to parents, carers and others who spend time with the child to help them best support the childs speach, language and communication needs

We also work with other people who spend time with the child. This might be parents, carers, school staff, child minders, grandparents, docters, nurses, physiotherapists or teachers of the deaf

Specialist therapist offers in swindon

AAS (Alternative and Augmentative Communication) - This refers to a range of techniques of communication for children who find using their voice difficult. This includes signing/ gestures, symbols/ pictures, communication boards/ books and voice output communication devices. Children who attend Special schools and need AAC are usually supported by the therapist who is based in that school, but they may also seek advice from the AAC specialist therapist. Children in mainstream schools and early years settings who need AAC are usually seen on the AAC specialist caseload. They work closely with the Advisory Teacher for Assistive Technology and also Occupational Therapists.

Autism Spectrum Conditions: Children with ASC may be seen in a range of settings, including, home, early years or school settings.

The Local Authority Designated Officer (LADO) is the person who should be notified when it has been alleged that a professional or volunteer who works with children has harmed the child or possibly committed an offence on or against a child etc
